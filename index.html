A UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of nature's most extraordinary creations

The Sunderban National Park stands as one of nature's most extraordinary creations, a UNESCO World Heritage Site that spans approximately 10,000 square kilometers across India and Bangladesh.

This magnificent tidal halophytic mangrove forest represents the largest of its kind in the world, creating a mystical landscape where land and water dance together in perfect harmony. Named after the majestic Sundari trees (Heritiera fomes) that dominate this unique ecosystem.

Sunderban is where the mighty Ganges, Brahmaputra, and Meghna rivers converge with the Bay of Bengal. This convergence creates a dynamic tapestry of islands, mudflats, and intricate waterways that transform with each tide, offering visitors an ever-changing panorama of natural beauty.

An Untamed Wilderness

Located in Madhya Pradesh, Sanjay Dubri National Park is part of the larger Sanjay-Dubri Tiger Reserve. This biodiverse region features six distinct safari zones, each offering unique wildlife sightings amidst dense forests and open grasslands. The park serves as a crucial corridor for wildlife movement between Madhya Pradesh and Chhattisgarh.

01

Dubri Core Zone

The prime tiger habitat with dense sal forests, known for regular tiger sightings and home to wild dogs and sloth bears.

02

Sanjay Zone

Features mixed vegetation with bamboo brakes, ideal for spotting leopards and over 150 bird species.

03

Singhori Zone

Vast grasslands and water bodies attract herds of chital and sambar deer, making it a prime hunting ground.

04

Parsili Zone

nown for scenic rocky outcrops and seasonal streams, offering sightings of Indian gaurs and wild boars.

05

Ranimagra Zone

Remote zone with pristine forests, providing habitat for rare species like flying squirrels and pangolins.

06

Buffer Zone

Relaxed safari regulations, offering full-day safaris and better wildlife photography opportunities.

A Glimpse into Panna's Rich History

From ancient temples to royal legacies, explore the historical treasures of Panna.

Origins & Establishment

Pench National Park, spanning the states of Madhya Pradesh and Maharashtra, derives its name from the Pench River that bisects the reserve. The area's conservation history dates back to 1977 when it was first declared a wildlife sanctuary.

The park inspired Rudyard Kipling's "The Jungle Book," making it one of India's most iconic wildlife destinations that continues to captivate visitors worldwide.

Ecological Significance

Pench represents one of central India's most vital tiger habitats, forming part of the Satpura-Maikal tiger landscape. The park's 758 square kilometers protect a diverse ecosystem of teak-dominated forests, grasslands, and riverine habitats.
